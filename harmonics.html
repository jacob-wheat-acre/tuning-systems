Lissajous Comma Piano Origins

Origins of the Western Scale

How a vibrating string became twelve notes — and why it almost didn't work.

1The Harmonic Series

A string never plays just one note.

When a string vibrates at 110 Hz, it simultaneously vibrates at every integer multiple of that frequency: 220, 330, 440, 550 Hz and beyond. These are called harmonics or partials. You hear them blended as a single timbre, but they're all present — and every musical interval we use traces back to the ratios between them.

Tap any string to hear that partial.
2Intervals from Ratios

The gaps between partials are the intervals we know.

Each adjacent pair of partials has a frequency ratio. The simpler the ratio, the more consonant the interval — because simpler ratios produce waveforms that lock into a stable, repeating pattern. These five ratios are where every tuning system begins.

3Building a Scale

Two ratios are enough to build a complete scale.

Pythagoras (~550 BCE) realized you only need 2:1 (the octave) and 3:2 (the perfect fifth) to derive a full set of pitches. Stack a fifth above any note, then fold back into the same octave by halving. After five steps you have the pentatonic scale; after seven, the familiar major scale.

Step 0 of 12 — start at C

Each note is added by multiplying its predecessor by 3/2, then halving if the result exceeds the octave. At step 5 (pentatonic) and step 7 (diatonic) the notes cluster into recognizable scale patterns. Continue to step 12 and you nearly return to the starting pitch — but not quite.

4The Problem

12 fifths should equal 7 octaves. They don't.

After 12 fifths you expect to land back on the starting note, seven octaves higher. Instead you overshoot by 23.46 cents — about a quarter of a semitone — called the Pythagorean comma. Every tuning system in history is a different answer to the same question: where do you hide this gap?

5Historical Solutions

Five centuries of compromise.

Each era solved the comma problem differently — trading purity in some intervals for usability across more keys.

~550 BCE – Renaissance

Pythagorean

All fifths pure (3:2). The full comma is dumped onto one "wolf" fifth that composers simply avoided.

Pure fifths Harsh thirds (81:64) Wolf fifth
~1480s

Just Intonation

Adds the 5:4 major third from partial 5. Chords are beatless and pure — but every key needs its own retuning.

Pure thirds (5:4) Pure fifths Can't modulate keys
~1520s

Meantone

Shrink every fifth slightly (by ¼ comma) to make major thirds nearly pure. Works in common keys; wolf persists.

Near-pure thirds Wolf fifth Remote keys unusable
~1690s

Well Temperament

Distribute the comma unevenly. Each key sounds slightly different — purer, brighter, or darker. Bach's WTC was written to celebrate this variety.

All 24 keys usable Distinct key characters Non-uniform intervals
Standardized ~1800s

Equal Temperament

Divide the octave into 12 perfectly equal semitones, each a ratio of 2^(1/12) ≈ 1.05946. The comma is spread evenly — every fifth is 1.955 cents narrower than pure, every major third is 13.69 cents wider than 5:4. No interval is pure, but every key is identically slightly out of tune. All fixed-pitch instruments today use this.

All keys identical Free modulation No pure intervals (except octave) No key color
6What We Gained and Lost

Equal temperament is a tradeoff, not a solution.

The 1.955-cent error in a single ET fifth is nearly imperceptible. But in a major chord, the third is 13.69 cents sharp — wide enough to produce audible beating between the two notes. The western scale is nature's harmonic series filtered through two thousand years of practical compromise. The 12 notes we have are not inevitable — they're the answer that worked well enough for everything else music wanted to do.
